This guide explains, in simple terms, how the EdNet KT1 dataset was cleaned and prepared for
analysis. The EdNet KT1 dataset contains records of students interacting with an online learning
platform — things like which questions they answered, whether they got them right, and how long
they spent on each question.
Before we could analyse anything, the raw data needed to be organised and checked for errors.
This document walks through exactly what was done and why.
What Did the Raw Data Look Like?
The raw data was a large table of individual interactions. Every row represented one moment a
student answered a question. It included:
• Which student answered (student ID)
• Which question they answered (question ID)
• Whether they got it right or wrong
• How long they took to answer (in milliseconds)
• What day of their learning journey it was
The problem with this format is that one student could have thousands of rows. To make it useful for
analysis, we needed to summarise each student into a single row with meaningful numbers.
What Did We Do to Clean It?
We went through several steps to turn the raw interaction logs into a clean, analysis-ready table.
Here is each step explained simply:
Step 1 — We Decided on a 30-Day Window
We chose to only look at the first 30 days of each student's activity. This is equivalent to one
calendar month, which is a sensible and standard period for measuring learning engagement. Any
interactions recorded after day 30 were excluded from most calculations.
Step 2 — We Created Summary Scores for Each Student
For each student, we calculated 9 summary scores (called features). Think of these like a student's
report card — each number tells us something different about how they used the platform:
• Multimedia Engagement — how much of their time was spent on multimedia content
• Performance Gain — did they improve? Measured by comparing early vs late accuracy
• Prior Achievement — how well they performed in their first week (days 1–7)
• Consistency — how steady their daily accuracy was from day to day
• Early Struggle — how well they performed in their first 3 days
• Skill Coverage — how many different question topics they attempted
• Average Session Duration — the average time spent per question in milliseconds
• Total Interactions — the total number of questions they attempted overall
• Peer Collaboration — the proportion of the 30-day window during which they were active
Step 3 — We Checked for Missing Values
After building the summary table, we checked every column for missing values. Any student row
with missing data was removed. After this check, the final dataset had zero missing values across
all columns — every student had a complete set of scores.
Step 4 — We Validated the Numbers
We ran an automatic check to make sure all numbers were within sensible ranges. For example,
any score representing a proportion should be between 0 and 1 — never higher. This check caught
two problems.
Problems Found and How We Fixed Them
Problem 1 — Peer Collaboration Was Going Above 1.0
What went wrong: Some students had values like 3.63 or 1.67 for peer
collaboration. A proportion cannot be greater than 1.0.
Why it happened: The calculation was counting interactions from days 31, 40, 50
and beyond — outside our 30-day window. Students with a lot of late activity looked
falsely over-engaged.
How we fixed it: We added a filter to only count days within the first 30 days, then
added a safety cap so the result can never exceed 1.0.
